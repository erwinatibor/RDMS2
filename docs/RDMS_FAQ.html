Services and Programs

Oral Health — General Public

Q: How do I keep my teeth healthy?

A: Brush with fluoride toothpaste for two minutes twice a day, floss daily, reduce sugary food and drink, drink water, avoid tobacco, and visit a dentist every six months.

Q: What causes tooth decay?

A: Tooth decay is caused by bacteria in the mouth that produce acids when they feed on sugars from food and drink. These acids erode the enamel. Poor brushing and high sugar diets increase risk.

Q: What causes gum disease?

A: Gum disease is caused by plaque buildup on teeth and gums. Risk factors include poor oral hygiene, smoking, diabetes, dry mouth, and certain medications.

Q: What are the signs of gum disease?

A: Signs of gum disease include red or swollen gums, bleeding when brushing, persistent bad breath, receding gums, tooth sensitivity, and loose teeth.

Q: Is gum disease linked to heart disease?

A: Yes. Research shows that bacteria from infected gums can enter the bloodstream and contribute to inflammation in the arteries, increasing the risk of heart disease and stroke.

Q: Can gum disease affect my blood sugar?

A: Yes. Gum disease can make blood sugar harder to control in people with diabetes. Treating gum disease has been shown to improve glucose management.

Q: Is bad breath a dental problem?

A: In most cases, yes. Persistent bad breath is usually caused by bacteria on the tongue, gum disease, dry mouth, or dental decay. A dentist can identify the cause.

Q: When should I see a dentist?

A: See a dentist if you have tooth pain, bleeding gums, swelling, a loose tooth, white or red patches in the mouth that do not heal, difficulty chewing, or sensitivity that persists. Routine check-ups every six months are recommended for everyone.

Q: What is a dental extraction?

A: A dental extraction is the removal of a tooth. It is performed when a tooth is too damaged to save, infected, or causing problems. It is done under local anesthesia and is not painful during the procedure.

Q: How often should I visit a dentist?

A: A routine dental check-up every six months is recommended for adults and children. People with gum disease, high cavity risk, or other conditions may need more frequent visits.

Q: Is tooth sensitivity normal?

A: Occasional sensitivity is common, but persistent sensitivity to hot, cold, or sweet foods can indicate worn enamel, exposed roots, or a cracked tooth. A dentist should evaluate persistent sensitivity.

Q: What is fluoride and why does it matter?

A: Fluoride is a mineral that strengthens tooth enamel and protects against decay. It is found in toothpaste, some drinking water, and can be applied directly by a dentist during a cleaning.

Q: Can oral health affect pregnancy?

A: Yes. Gum disease during pregnancy is linked to premature birth and low birth weight. Pregnant women should maintain good oral hygiene and continue dental check-ups.

Children and Oral Health

Q: When should a child first see a dentist?

A: A child should have their first dental visit by age one or within six months of the first tooth appearing.

Q: How do I clean my baby's teeth?

A: Before teeth appear, clean the gums with a soft damp cloth. Once teeth appear, use a soft baby toothbrush with a rice-sized amount of fluoride toothpaste.

Q: At what age can children brush alone?

A: Children should be supervised when brushing until at least age seven or eight. Before that age, they lack the coordination to brush effectively on their own.

Q: What causes early childhood tooth decay?

A: Putting babies to sleep with a bottle containing milk, juice, or sweetened liquids is a primary cause of early childhood tooth decay, also known as baby bottle tooth decay.

Q: Are baby teeth important?

A: Yes. Baby teeth hold space for permanent teeth, support speech development, and are used for chewing. Tooth decay in baby teeth can cause pain, infection, and problems with permanent teeth.

Q: What are dental sealants?

A: Dental sealants are thin coatings applied to the chewing surfaces of back teeth to protect them from decay. They are recommended for school-age children and are a safe, effective preventive measure.
